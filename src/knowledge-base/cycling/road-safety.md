# Road Safety for Cyclists

## Visibility is Survival
On Indian roads, cyclists are among the most vulnerable. Being seen is your first line of defence.

**Mandatory Gear**:
- **Helmet**: Always. No exceptions. ISI or CE certified. Budget: Btwin 100 (Rs 500). Mid: Giro Register (Rs 3,500)
- **Front light**: White, minimum 300 lumens for day, 800+ for night. Keeps flashing mode on during daytime
- **Rear light**: Red, flashing mode. Visible from 500 metres. Mount on seat post
- **Reflective vest or jacket**: Especially important during dawn, dusk, and overcast conditions
- **Reflective tape**: Apply strips on frame, pedals, and helmet

## Hand Signals
Communicate your intentions clearly to drivers behind you:
- **Left turn**: Left arm extended straight
- **Right turn**: Right arm extended straight (India rides on the left, so right turns cross traffic)
- **Stopping**: Left arm extended downward, palm facing back
- **Hazard on road**: Point at the ground on the side of the hazard
- **Slowing down**: Repeated patting motion with left hand, palm down

## Traffic Rules for Cyclists
- Ride on the **left side** of the road, in the direction of traffic
- Use cycle lanes where available (major cities are adding these)
- Obey all traffic signals -- red lights apply to cyclists too
- Never ride against traffic flow (wrong-side riding)
- Avoid riding in the blind spots of trucks and buses
- Make eye contact with drivers at intersections before proceeding

## Night Riding
Ride at night only when necessary. If you must:
- Use a front light with minimum 800 lumens on steady mode
- Rear light on flash mode
- Wear reflective clothing on all limbs (moving reflectors are more noticeable)
- Stick to well-lit, familiar routes
- Reduce speed -- potholes and road hazards are harder to spot

## Group Cycling on Roads
- Ride in a maximum of two-abreast formation; single file on narrow or busy roads
- Maintain 1-metre gap between riders
- Lead rider calls out hazards: "Hole!", "Car back!", "Slowing!"
- Never overlap wheels with the rider ahead -- the leading cause of group crashes
- Use a sweep rider with a phone to manage emergencies from the back
