# Understanding and Preventing Altitude Sickness

## What is AMS?
Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) occurs when your body struggles to adapt to lower oxygen levels at high elevations. It can affect anyone regardless of fitness level and is the leading cause of trekking emergencies in the Himalayas.

## Altitude Thresholds
- **Below 8,000 ft (2,400 m)**: Low risk. Most people feel fine.
- **8,000 - 12,000 ft (2,400 - 3,600 m)**: Moderate risk. Acclimatize for 1 day per 1,000 ft gain above 8,000 ft.
- **12,000 - 15,000 ft (3,600 - 4,500 m)**: High risk. Mandatory rest days every 2,000 ft gain. Sleep low, hike high.
- **Above 15,000 ft (4,500 m)**: Extreme risk. Only attempt after proper acclimatization. Watch for HACE/HAPE symptoms.

## Symptoms of AMS (Recognize Early)
- Headache that does not respond to paracetamol
- Nausea and loss of appetite
- Dizziness and fatigue
- Difficulty sleeping
- Shortness of breath during rest (not just exertion)

## Severe Conditions (Life-Threatening)
- **HACE** (High Altitude Cerebral Edema): Confusion, inability to walk straight, hallucinations
- **HAPE** (High Altitude Pulmonary Edema): Persistent cough, pink/frothy sputum, extreme breathlessness at rest

## Acclimatization Schedule
1. Above 8,000 ft, do not ascend more than 1,000 ft per day (sleeping altitude)
2. Take a rest day every 3,000 ft gained
3. "Climb high, sleep low" -- hike to a higher point during the day but return to a lower camp to sleep
4. Stay hydrated -- drink 3-4 litres of water daily

## Medication
- **Diamox (Acetazolamide)**: 125 mg twice daily, starting 24 hours before ascent. Consult your doctor first. Common side effects include tingling in fingers and increased urination.
- Carry Dexamethasone for emergency use (prescription only)

## When to Descend IMMEDIATELY
- Symptoms worsen despite rest and medication
- Any signs of HACE or HAPE
- SpO2 drops below 80% (carry a pulse oximeter)
- **Descend at least 1,000-3,000 ft and seek medical help**
